Likelihood and linear models exercises

1 Likelihood exercises

  1. Let \(f:\mathbb{R}\to\mathbb{R}\) be defined by \(f(\theta)=e^{-\theta^2+4\theta}\).
    1. Find the first derivative of \(f\), and hence identify its turning point(s).
    2. Calculate the value of the second derivative of \(f\) at these turning point(s). Hence, deduce if the turning point(s) are local maxima or local minima.
  1. Suppose that we have a biased coin, which throws a head with probability \(\theta\in[0,1]\), and a tail with probability \(1-\theta\). The coin flips are independent of each other. We toss the coin \(n\) times, and record the total number of heads.
    1. Let \(X\) be the total number of heads thrown after \(n\) tosses. Write down the distribution of \(X\).
    2. Write down the likelihood function \(L(\theta;x)\) of \(X\), where \(x\) is the total number of heads in \(n\) tosses. State the range of values taken by \(\theta\).
    3. Suppose that in \(n=10\) tosses we throw \(x=7\) heads. In R, plot the likelihood function, and verify visually that the maximum is at \(\theta = 7/10\). Use the curve() function for plotting a function, and abline(v = 0.7) for drawing a vertical line at \(\theta = 7/10\).
  1. A sample \((x_1,x_2,x_3)=(4,0,3)\) of three independent observations is obtained from a Poisson distribution with parameter \(\lambda\). Here, \(\lambda\) is known to be in \(\Lambda=\{1,2,3\}\). Find the likelihood of each of the possible values of \(\lambda\), and hence find the maximum likelihood estimator.
  1. A sample \((x_1,x_2)=(3, 1)\) of two independent observations is obtained from a normal distribution with mean \(\mu\) and variance 1.
    1. Write down the likelihood function for \(\mu\).
    2. In R, plot the likelihood function for \(\mu\) ranging between -1 and 5, and verify visually that the maximum is at \(\mu = 2\).
  1. Consider a sequence of i.i.d. samples \(\mathbf{x}=(x_1,x_2,\ldots,x_n)\) from the normal distribution \(N(\mu,\theta)\), where \(\mu\) is known.
    1. Write down the likelihood function \(L(\theta;\mathbf{x})\), and specify the range of values \(\Theta\) taken by \(\theta\).
    2. Find the log likelihood \(\ell(\theta;\mathbf{x})\), and simplify it as much as you can.
    3. Find the maximum likelihood estimator \(\hat\theta\).
    4. Is the maximum likelihood estimator biased in this case?
  1. A sequence of i.i.d. samples \(\mathbf{x}=(x_1,x_2,\ldots,x_n)\) is taken from an inverse Gaussian distribution (known also as the Wald distribution) which has the p.d.f. \[f(x)=\sqrt{\frac{\theta}{2\pi x^3}} \exp\left(-\frac{\theta(x-\mu)^2}{2\mu^2x}\right) \quad \text{ when }x>0,\] and \(f(x)=0\) for \(x\leq 0\), with parameters \(\mu,\theta>0\).

Both \(\mu\) and \(\theta\) are unknown. Find the maximum likelihood estimator of \((\mu,\theta)\).

  1. Let \(\mathbf{x}=(x_1,\ldots,x_n)\) be a sequence of i.i.d. samples of the random variable \(X\), and suppose that the distribution of \(X\) has the parameters \(\theta\).
    1. Let \(\ell\) be the corresponding log likelihood function. Show that \[\ell(\boldsymbol\theta;\mathbf{x})=\sum_{i=1}^n \log f_X(x_i;\boldsymbol\theta).\]
    2. Write \(\phi(x;\,\mu,v)\) for the probability density function of the \(N(\mu,v)\) distribution, with parameters \(\boldsymbol\theta=(\mu,v)\). Let \(X\sim logN(\mu, v)\). Show that for \(x>0\) \[f_X(x;\,\mu,v)=\frac{1}{x}\phi(\log x;\,\mu,v).\] Find the corresponding log-likelihood function \(\ell(\mu,v;\,\mathbf{x})\), in terms of \(\phi\).
    3. Without doing any further calculations, combine your results from (a) and (b) to deduce formulae for the maximum likelihood estimator of \((\mu,v)\).
  1. Suppose that we have a sequence of i.i.d. samples \(\mathbf{x}=(x_1,\ldots,x_n)\) taken from a \(N(\mu,\sigma^2)\) distribution, where \(\mu\) is unknown and \(\sigma^2\) is known. Suppose that \(n=144\), \(\sigma^2=1\) and the data satisfies \(\bar{x}=2.05\). Does a \(2\)-likelihood test support the hypothesis that the true mean is \(\mu=2\)?
  1. Suppose we tossed a coin \(n=10\) times and observed \(x=7\) heads. We model the number of heads \(X\) as a \(Bi(n,\theta)\) distribution with \(n=10\) and unknown \(\theta\).
    1. Use R to verify that \(R_2\), the range of values for \(\theta\) which the log likelihood is within \(2\) of its maximum value, is given approximately by \([0.388, 0.918]\).
    2. In this situation, an approximate 95% confidence interval is given by \(\hat{\theta}\pm 1.96\sqrt{\frac{\hat{\theta}(1-\hat{\theta})}{n}},\) where \(\hat{\theta}=x/n\). Compare this confidence interval to your answer in (a).
    3. Repeat this analysis with \(n=100\) and \(x=70\). (You do not need to compute the 2-likelihood region here. This can be obtained numerically as, approximately, [0.60,0.79].)
  1. Let \(\mathbf{x}=(x_1,\ldots,x_n)\) be a sequence of i.i.d. samples from the \(Beta(\theta,1)\) distribution, where \(\theta\) is unknown. Show that the \(k\)-likelihood region for \(\theta\) is \[R_k=\left\{\theta\in(0,\infty):\left|\frac{(\hat\theta-\theta)}{\hat\theta}+\log(\theta/\hat\theta)\right|\leq \frac{k}{n}\right\},\] where \(\hat\theta\) denotes the maximum likelihood estimator of \(\theta\).
