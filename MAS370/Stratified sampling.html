12  Stratified Sampling: Introduction

If we have information about the structure of a population it may be possible to use this information to improve on the straightforward SRS estimator. In this chapter we look at when and how extra information can be used in this way.

Suppose the population is made up of identifiable subsets or strata. For example:

  1. different geographical regions;

  2. different socio-economic groups.

Rather than drawing a SRS from the whole population, we might wish to ensure representation from each stratum â€“ for example, by drawing a SRS independently from each and combining in such a way as to get a better representation of the whole population. Call this form of sampling Stratified Simple Random Sampling (SSRS).

Reasons why SSRS might be desirable:

  1. it can lead to greater precision of estimation;

  2. strata may be of interest in their own right;

  3. it may be administratively convenient â€“ for example, field offices can administer the survey in their own regions;

  4. there may be different sampling problems in different strata â€“ for example, people in institutions such as hospitals, prisons, universities may need different approaches;

  5. post-sampling stratification can adjust for an unrepresentative sample.

12.1 Notation

Suppose there are \(l\) strata, with sizes \(N_i, i=1,\dots,l\), so that \(\sum_1^lN_i=N\). Members of \(i\)th stratum denoted by \(X_{i,j}, j=1,\dots,N_i\).

The population mean of the \(i\)th stratum is \(\overline{X}_i\) and its variance is \[S_i^2=\frac{1}{N_i-1} \sum_{j=1}^{N_i}(X_{i,j}-\overline{X}_i)^2.\] Then the population mean is \[\overline{X}=\frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^l\sum_{j=1}^{N_i}X_{i,j}=\sum_{i=1}^l\frac{N_i}{N}\overline{X}_i,\] is a weighted mean of the stratum means \(\overline{X}_i\), and \[ S^2 =\frac{1}{N-1} \sum_{i=1}^l \sum_{j=1}^{N_i}(X_{i,j}-\overline{X})^2\]

\[= \frac{1}{N-1} \left\{\sum_{i=1}^l (N_i-1)S_i^2+\sum_{i=1}^lN_i (\overline{X}_i - \overline{X})^2 \right\} \tag{12.1}\] where inside the curly brackets we have a decomposition of the total sum of squares of the population values about their mean into a pooled within-stratum sum of squares and a between-strata sum of squares, identical algebraically to that found in the one-way analysis of variance. We will use this result in Section 12.3.

Suppose we draw a SRS of size \(n_i\) from the \(i\)th stratum, for \(i=1,\dots,l\). Let \(n=\sum_1^l n_i\) denote the size of the combined sample, and \(f_i=n_i/N_i\) the sampling fraction for stratum \(i\), \(i=1,\dots,l\). Let \(x_{ij}, j=1,\dots,n_i\) denote the observations from the SRS from stratum \(i\), and \(\overline{x}_i, s_i^2\) the corresponding sample mean and variance. Initially we take \(n\) and the \(n_i\) to be fixed.

12.2 Estimation of \(\overline{X}\)

The overall sample mean \(\overline{x}^\prime = \sum_i\sum_j x_{ij}/n = \sum_i n_i \overline{x}_i/n\) has expectation \[E(\overline{x}^\prime )=\frac{1}{n} \sum_1^l n_i \overline{X}_i\] evidently equal to \(\overline{X}\) if \(n_i = nN_i/N\), and so \(\overline{x}^\prime\) in general is biased for \(\overline{X}\), except if \(f_i = \mbox{constant} = n/N\), the so-called proportional allocation of sampling effort (the size of the SRS from each stratum is proportional to the stratum size).

This suggests a better unbiased estimate, the stratified estimate using proportional allocation: \[\overline{x}_{st} = \frac{1}{N} \sum_1^l N_i \overline{x}_i.\]

It can be proven that whatever the \(n_i,i=1,\dots,l\), under SSRS \(\overline{x}_{st}\) is the best linear unbiased estimator (BLUE) of \(\overline{X}\), and it has variance \[ \mbox{var}(\overline{x}_{st}) = \sum_1^l\left(\frac{N_i}{N}\right)^2\frac{1-f_i}{n_i} S_i^2. \tag{12.2}\] Usually the stratum population variances \(S_i^2\) are unknown, and so they are estimated by the corresponding stratum sample variances \(s_i^2\), which by SRS are unbiased. Confidence intervals for \(\overline{X}\) may then be based on a normal approximation in the usual way.

This is quite a hard exercise! You wonâ€™t be asked prove results such as this in the exam.

Exercise 12.1 Prove Equation 12.2. You may quote results for SRS. (For completeness, the solution to this exercise will also show that \(\overline{x}_{st}\) is BLUE).

Solution

We have \[ Var( \bar{x}_{st}) =Var\left(\frac{1}{N}\sum_1^l N_i\bar{x}_i\right)= \frac{1}{N^2}\sum_1^lVar(N_i\bar{x}_i) = \sum_1^l\left(\frac{N_i}{N}\right)^2\frac{1 - f_i}{n_i}S_i^2, \] as \(\bar{x_i}\) is the sample mean obtained from an SRS of size \(n_i\) drawn from a population of size \(N_i\) with population variance \(S_i^2\).

It is also possible to show that \(\bar{x}_{st}\) is the best linear unbiased estimator (BLUE). Consider a general linear estimator of \(\overline{X}\) \[ t = \sum_{i,j} c_{ij}x_{ij} \] where the \(c_{ij}\) are constants.

Unbiasedness implies \[\begin{align} Et & = \sum_{ij} c_{ij} \overline{X}_i \\ & = \sum_{i=1}^l \left(\sum_{j=1}^{n_i} c_{ij}\right) \overline{X}_{i} \\ & = \overline{X} = \sum_{i=1}^l \left(\frac{N_i}{N}\right) \overline{X}_i\mbox{ whatever}\overline{X}_i \\ \mbox{and so }\sum_{j=1}^{n_i} c_{ij} & = \frac{N_i}{N} \mbox{ for each }i. \end{align}\] Since the SRSâ€™s from different strata are independent,

\[ Var(t) =\sum_{i=1}^lVar\left(\sum_{j=1}^{n_i} c_{ij}x_{ij}\right)\] and we minimise this by minimising it for each \(i\) separately. But within each stratum this is essentially the same problem as for SRS , and the solution is that the \(c_{ij}\) should be equal to each other, which means, bearing in mind the above constraint, that \[c_{ij} = \frac{N_i}{Nn_i}\mbox{ for }j=1,\ldots ,n_i\] and so in SSRS the BLUE of \(\overline X\) is \[\overline{x}_{st} = \sum_{i=1}^l \frac{N_i}{N} \overline{x}_i. \]

Example

Example 12.1 Sheffield University wants to estimate the mean CO\(_2\) transport emissions of its staff. The university has 2000 employees. Three transport mode strata have been identified: car, bus/tram/train and walk/cycle. The strata sizes are 200(car), 500(bus/tram/train) and 1300 (walk/cycle). A stratified SRS of 100 is to be taken. Using proportional allocation, the sample sizes are 10, 25 and 65 respectively. The results of the sample are as follows:

Sample mean (tonnes) Sample variance (tonnes\(^2\))
car 0.82 0.35
bus/tram/train 0.28 0.26
walk/cycle 0.13 0.02

Calculate a 95% CI for the mean CO\(_2\) transport emissions of its staff based on these results. \[\overline{x}_{st}=0.1(0.82)+0.25(0.28)+0.65(0.13)=0.24 ~\text{tonnes of CO}_2.\] Using proportional allocation we have \(f_i=n/N=100/2000=0.05\) which is small enough to ignore. This gives the estimator variance as: \[\text{var}(\overline{x}_{st}) = (0.1)^2\frac{0.35}{10}+(0.25)^2\frac{0.26}{25}+(0.65)^2\frac{0.02}{65}=0.0011\] The 95% CI is then \[\overline{x}_{st} \pm 2 \sqrt{\text{var}(\overline{x}_{st})}=0.24 \pm 2\sqrt{0.0011}=(0.17, 0.31)\]

12.3 Stratification vs SRS

When is \(\overline{x}_{st}\) better than \(\overline{x}\) from SRS? Both are unbiased, and so we compare variances. Using Equation 11.1 and Equation 12.2, it can then be shown that \(\mbox{var}(\overline{x})\geq\mbox{var}(\overline{x}_{st})\) requires \[ \sum N_i(\overline{X}_i - \overline{X})^2 \geq\sum \left(1-\frac{N_i}{N}\right) S_i^2. \tag{12.3}\]

The term on the left hand side is the between-strata sum of squares, and the one on the right hand side is a measure of the within-stratum variance. Thus \(\overline{x}_{st}\) will have smaller variance than \(\overline{x}\), i.e. SSRS will be preferable to SRS, when the strata are homogeneous (small within-stratum variance) but well-separated (large difference between stratum means).

Again, you wonâ€™t be asked to prove results like this in the exam, but this is a little more straightfoward, using the equations suggested.

Exercise 12.2 Prove Equation 12.3 using Equation 12.1, Equation 11.1 and Equation 12.2.

Solution

We have \[ Var(\overline{x}_{st(p)}) = \sum_1^l \left( \frac{N_i}{N} \right)^2 \left(1-\frac{n}{N}\right)\frac{S_i^2}{n_i} = \frac{1-f}{nN} \sum_1^l N_iS_i^2 \] whereas, following the hint, \[Var(\overline{x})= \frac{1-f}{n}S^2 = \frac{1-f}{n(N-1)} \left\{ \sum_{i=1}^l (N_i-1)S_i^2 + \sum_{i=1}^l N_i(\overline{X}_i - \overline{X})^2 \right\} . \]
Thus \[Var(\overline{x})-Var(\overline{x}_{st(p)})=\frac{1-f}{n(N-1)} \left\{-\sum_{i=1}^l\frac{N-N_i}{N}S_i^2 + \sum_{i=1}^lN_i(\overline{X}_i - \overline{X})^2 \right\} \] which is non-negative iff \[\sum N_i(\overline{X}_i - \overline{X})^2 \geq \sum \left(1-\frac{N_i}{N}\right) S_i^2. \]

12.4 Choice of Sample Sizes \(n, n_1,\dots,n_l\)

As before, the larger the samples within strata, the more precise the estimation. However there is usually a cost associated with sampling that inhibits use of very large samples. The best values will depend therefore on the detailed costs of sampling in relation to the value of precision of estimation. The following captures the main features of many cases, at least approximately.

Suppose that the cost of the survey consists of a basic overhead cost, \(c_0\) say, and a cost associated with each unit measured. This unit measurement cost could differ between strata. We denote its value in stratum \(i\) by \(c_i\). Then the overall cost, \(C\) say, of the survey is of the form \[C = c_0+\sum_{i=1}^l c_in_i.\]

Suppose we wish to choose the \(n_i\) to minimise var \(\overline{x}_{st}\) for a specified total cost \(C\). (We might equally wish to minimise cost for a specified variance: the argument in that case is similar.)

12.4.1 Minimising Variance Within a Fixed Cost.

We want to choose the \(n_i\) to minimize \[\begin{align} \mbox{var}(\overline{x}_{st}) & = \sum_1^l\left(\frac{N_i}{N}\right)^2\left(1-\frac{n_i}{N_i}\right)\frac{S_i^2}{n_i}\\ & = \sum_1^l \left(\frac{N_i}{N}\right)^2\frac{S_i^2}{n_i}-\sum_1^l \left(\frac{N_i}{N}\right)^2\frac{S_i^2}{N_i} \end{align}\] subject to \[c_0+\sum_1^l c_in_i = C.\] Using Lagrange multipliers, we find that the optimal size of sample from the \(i\)th stratum is \[n_i \propto \frac{N_iS_i}{\sqrt{c_i}}\mbox{~~~~for~}i=1,\dots,l.\] As one might expect, we should take a larger sample in a given stratum if

  1. the stratum is larger,

  2. the stratum is more variable internally,

  3. sampling is cheaper in the stratum.

Putting the formula for \(n_i\) in the cost constraint, we can deduce the constant of proportionality, and the optimal total number of observations for cost \(C\): \[\begin{align} n_i &=\frac{(C-c_0)\frac{N_iS_i}{\sqrt{c_i}}}{\sum_1^lN_iS_i\sqrt{c_i}},\\ n &= \frac{(C-c_0)\sum_1^l \frac{N_iS_i}{\sqrt{c_i}}}{\sum_1^lN_iS_i\sqrt{c_i}}. \end{align}\] (In practice we would round \(n\) and \(n_i\) to the nearest integers.) To use this result, we need to know the sizes \(N_i\) of the strata and the stratum variances \(S_i^2\). The latter are usually unknown and would have to be estimated from a pilot survey or from knowledge of another similar survey.

12.4.2 Special Case: \(c_i\) constant.

In the case when measurement of a unit costs the same whichever stratum it comes from, so that the overall cost is \(C=c_0+cn\), then \[\begin{align} n & = \frac{C-c_0}{c} \\ \mbox{and}\; n_i & = \frac{nN_iS_i}{\sum_1^l N_iS_i}\; \mbox{for} i=1,\dots,l. \end{align}\] This is called the Neyman allocation. For the Neyman allocation, we have \[\mbox{var~}\overline{x}_{st} = \frac{1}{N^2} \frac{ (\sum N_iS_i)^2}{n} - \frac{ \sum N_iS_i^2 }{N^2}.\]

Example

Example 12.2 A large village council wants to know the proportion of households that have good insulation in the village. They inspect properties in order to make this assessment. The inspection costs are different according to whether the property is in the centre of the village, on the outskirts or very remote.

Exercise 12.3 Show that if all the stratum variances are the same, the Neyman allocation is the same as proportional allocation.

Solution

If the stratum variances are all the same, we have \(S_i=K\) for some constant \(K\) for all \(i\), and so \[ n_i = \frac{nN_iS_i}{\sum N_iS_i} = \frac{nN_i}{\sum N_i} = \frac{nN_i}{N}, \] the same stratum sample size used in proportional allocation.

12.5 Stratification After Sampling

Often the stratum to which a unit belongs is unknown until after sampling (for example: age, educational level). If the \(N_i\) are known (e.g. from census) a possibility is to take an SRS and then classify units into strata and use as estimator of \(\overline{X}\) \[\overline{x}_w = \sum \left( \frac{N_i}{N}\right)\overline{x}_i\] where \(\overline{x}_i\) is the sample mean of the units that happened to be in stratum \(i\). This â€˜correctsâ€™ the sample so that it has the same profile as the population. The technical difference here compared to the standard stratified estimator is that the \(n_i\) are now random.

As an example, suppose we sample 100 people from the population to estimate average weight, but find sample contains only 17 men instead of the 50% in the population. The above suggests using \(\overline{x}_w = 0.5\overline{x}_{women} +0.5 \overline{x}_{men}\) instead of the usual SRS estimator \(\overline{x}\).

This is a good revision question.

  1. proportional allocation;

  2. Neyman allocation.

Compare the precision of these methods with that of simple random sampling.

Solution

For proportional allocation, take a proportion \(n/N=100/2010\) of each stratum, giving (on rounding to the nearest integer) \[ (n_1,\ldots ,n_7) = (20,23,19,17,8,6,7). \] For Neyman allocation, take \(n_i \propto N_iS_i\), giving \[ (n_1,\ldots ,n_7) = (10,18,17,19,12,9,15). \] The variances of the estimators of the mean (neglecting the effect of rounding) are given by the standard formulae, namely \[\begin{align} Var(\overline{x}_{st(p)}) & = \frac{1-f}{n}\sum \frac{N_iS_i^2}{N} \simeq 3.26 \\ \mbox{var~}\overline{x}_{st(o)} & = \frac{(\sum N_iS_i)^2}{nN^2} - \frac{\sum N_iS_i^2}{N^2} \simeq 2.72 \\ Var(\overline{x}) & = \frac{1-f}{n}S^2 = \frac{1-f}{n}~\frac{\sum (N_i-1)S_i^2 + \sum N_i(\overline{X}_i-\overline{X})^2}{N-1} \simeq 5.89 \end{align}\] which use the following data: \[ \sum N_iS_i = 34206.9, \quad\sum S_i^2 = 3381.12, \] \[\sum N_iS_i^2 = 689990.39, \quad\sum N_i(\overline{X}_i-\overline{X})^2 = 557693.86. \] The variances of the estimators of the total acreage are \(N^2\) times the above, namely \(13.18\times 10^6, 11.01\times 10^6\) and \(23.78\times 10^6\) respectively. Thus, the optimal allocation gives about a 16% improvement in variance compared with proportional allocation, and over a 50% reduction compared with SRS.
