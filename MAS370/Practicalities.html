15  Practicalities of Surveys: Problems at the Planning Stage

The following is a list, taken from (Moser and Kalton 1971) of some things that need to be borne in mind when planning a survey.

  1. Objectives and Resources: should be known precisely in advance.

  2. Coverage: target population, study population?

  3. Collection of data: how will it be done? – observation? mail? interview? web? etc

  4. Questionnaire: wording, scope?

  5. Errors: anticipate and take precautions.

  6. Fieldwork: eg needing staff – recruitment? training? supervision?

  7. Processing & Analysis: data entry, computing?

  8. Documents: questionnaires, record sheets etc.

  9. Timing, Cost.

15.1 Ethics and data protection

A survey conducted by staff or students at a UK university for research purposes would need ethics approval first, and other organisations may have similar requirements. A brief guide to ethics approval at Sheffield is available here.

Surveys may involve the collection of personal data. In the UK, there are legal requirements regarding the handling of personal data: the UK General Data Protection Regulation (UK GDPR) (similar to the European Union GDPR) and the Data Protection Act 2018. For an overview, see https://www.gov.uk/data-protection.

15.2 Questionnaire Design

Considerations:

  1. Introduction/instructions: should be clear and courteous.

  2. Resist the temptation to include too many questions: too many can be demoralizing, off-putting and likely to increase refusal rates.

  3. Questions: factual questions should be such that it is reasonable to expect an accurate answer. Example: How many hours have you spent on social media in the past week? And how much in the same week a year ago?

Questions about opinions have to recognise that the respondent may have many-sided, not necessarily logically coherent views, or may not have thought about the question at all. The wording or setting of the question can produce quite divergent results, making objective value debatable.

Example: attitude to bus priority: effect of wording of alternative. Survey of 2157 people in London.

  • Are you in favour of giving priority to buses in the rush hour? (69%) 

Or not? (31%)

  • Are you in favour of giving priority to buses in the rush hour? (55%)

Or should cars have just as much priority as buses? (B): 45%

So question effect makes a difference of \(\approx 14\%\) (s.e. 2%).

  1. Ambiguous questions. Some examples
  • Is your work made more difficult because you are expecting a baby?

Does NO mean ‘I’m not expecting’ or ‘Even if I were expecting my work wouldn’t be made more difficult’ ?

  • Do you like travelling on trains or buses?

Only one, or both?

  • How strongly do you agree (using a Likert scale) with the statement, “I do my best work every day”?

Do you mean ‘Every day I work productively and achieve all my goals’ (for example), or, ‘Every day, the work I do is better than any work I have done before’?

  1. Leading Questions:
  • Do you agree that it is unfair that \(\ldots\)?

15.3 Randomized Response Technique

This is a technique for estimating the population proportion with a specified characteristic without needing to know the status of individual respondents. It is useful for sensitive (embarrassing, incriminating \(\ldots\)) issues. It works by having two questions:

Q1 The sensitive question of interest, with a Yes/No answer. e.g. I have used intravenous drugs at some point in my life.

Q2 An innocuous question also with a Yes/No answer for which the population proportion of YES responses is known: e.g. Is your birthday in April?

The respondent is asked to choose Q1 with a known probability \(P\) and Q2 with probability \(1-P\), tossing a possibly biased coin to decide which. The respondent is assumed to answer truthfully whichever of the questions comes up, but no one else is told which of the questions it was.

If \(p\) is the proportion of YES answers observed in the sample, and \(\pi_1\) and \(\pi_2\) are the population proportions of YES answers to questions 1 and 2 respectively (so that \(\pi_1\) is required and \(\pi_2\) is known), then \[Ep=P \pi_1 + (1-P) \pi_2,\] whence an unbiased estimator of \(\pi_1\) is \[\widehat{\pi}_1 = \frac{p-(1-P)\pi_2}{P}.\\[2ex]\]

These questions are quite hard, but will help you understand how the randomised response method works.

Exercise 15.1  

  1. Derive the variance of \(\hat{\pi}_1\). Based on the variance and more general considerations, should \(P\) be as large as possible?

Hint: use the result that

\[ \frac{n(P\pi_1 + (1-P)\pi_2)(1 - P\pi_1 - (1-P)\pi_2)}{P^2}=n\left(\pi_1 + \frac{\pi_2}{P} - \pi_2\right)\left(\frac{1-\pi_2}{P} - \pi_1 + \pi_2\right), \] and think about what value of \(P\) minimises the expression on the right hand side.

  1. Suppose Q2 is replaced by the complement of Q1 (I have never used intravenous drugs). Derive an unbiased estimator of \(\pi_1\). What would happen if \(P=\frac{1}{2}\)?
