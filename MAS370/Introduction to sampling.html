10  Introduction to Sampling

This topic covers statistical ideas behind such things as Political Opinion Polls, the Labour Force Survey, Crime Surveys, the International Passenger Survey (IPS) etc. We are generally working with a finite population, and we wish to estimate some overall characteristic of the whole population on the basis of a sample. The characteristic might be quantitative or qualitative. Why do we choose to sample?

  • We may be able to get good estimates without a full census (cost effective).

  • There may be no alternative: testing may be destructive.

  • How should we select the sample – to optimise estimation in some sense?

  • How do we determine the sample size?

  • How do we draw valid conclusions from the sample and say how precise they are?

Examples of surveys

Name of survey Target population Information sought from sample members Population Characteristic of interest
Opinion Poll (e.g. YouGov polls) People aged 18 or over in the UK Which political party do you intend to vote for at the next general election? Population proportion voting for each party
International passenger survey (IPS) People entering and leaving UK in a given time period Nationality and length of visit Total numbers of immigrants and emigrants
Crime survey for England and Wales Population of England and Wales Have they been a victim of a particular crime? Proportion of population that have been victims of a particular crime

Poor methods:

  1. Judgemental sampling: deliberately subjective choice of items to make the sample ‘representative’. Drawbacks: hard to avoid unconscious selection biases, difficult to assess estimation precision.

  2. Accessibility sampling: choose items according to ease of access. Drawbacks: possible selection bias, difficult to assess estimation precision.

  3. Self-selecting sampling: send invite to all in study population and see who responds. Drawbacks: responder bias.

Better method:

  1. Probability/Random sampling: each member of the population has a known (not necessarily equal) probability of being selected. For this, the drawbacks above disappear.

  2. Simple Random Sampling: all possible samples have equal probability of being selected.

Questions to address:

  • methods of sampling (simple, stratified & cluster sampling);

  • properties of estimators (precision, whether they are unbiased);

  • implementation (sample size, questionnaire design).

10.1 A classic example of the problems with self-selecting sampling

Before we study ‘proper’ sampling methods, it is first worth discussing ‘self-selecting’ sampling. The technique is actually quite old, but has perhaps become more popular given the ease of conducting such surveys via the internet. The idea is to send an invitation to everyone in the study population, inviting them to take part in a survey. For example, readers of a website are invited to submit their views on an issue. There is obvious potential for bias, as those with stronger views may be more likely to respond, and the study population receiving the invitation may not be representative of the target population in any case. An old example of how this can go wrong is given on the website (Kellner 2018).

“A biased sample is a biased sample, however large it is. One celebrated example of this was the US Presidential Election in 1936. A magazine, Literary Digest, sent out 10 million post cards asking people how they would vote, received almost 2.3 million back and said that Alfred Landon was leading Franklin Roosevelt by 57-43 per cent. The Digest did not gather information that would allow it to judge the quality of its sample and correct, or”weight”, groups that were under- or over-represented. A young pollster called George Gallup employed a much smaller sample (though, at 50,000, it was much larger than those normally used today), but because he ensured that it was representative, he correctly showed Roosevelt on course to win by a landslide. In the event, Roosevelt won 60% and Landon just 37%. The Literary Digest closed down soon afterwards.”

In some cases, it may be possible to adjust for bias if the appropriate covariates are recorded (e.g.in an opinion poll for voting intention, we might ask for how the respondent previously voted). Typically, though, self-selecting samples aren’t reliable for inferring target population characteristics. They can, however, be useful for tracking changes in characteristics within particular groups.

10.2 Notation

Consider a finite population of \(N\) units.

  • The \(N\) population values are represented by \(X_1, \ldots ,X_N\)

  • The \(n\) sampled values are represented by \(x_1, \ldots ,x_n\)

  • The proportion of the population sampled (the Sampling fraction) is \(f=n/N\)

  • The population mean (usual quantity of interest) is \(\overline{X} = \sum_{1}^{N}X_{i}/N\)

  • The population variance is \[\begin{align*} S^{2} &= \frac{1}{N-1} \sum_{1}^{N} (X_{i}-\overline{X})^{2}\\ &=\frac{1}{N-1} \left(\sum_{1}^{N} X_{i}^2- N\overline{X}^{2}\right) \end{align*}\]

Important

Note that the notational convention for random and non-random variables here is the opposite to that more commonly used: here capitals (\(X_1, \ldots ,X_N\)) denote fixed population values and lower case letters \(x_1, \ldots ,x_n\) denoting the sampled values are the random variables.

10.3 Appendix: finite and infinite populations

The following discussion is not examinable, but is provided as background.

What does it mean in practice when we distinguish between “finite” and “infinite” populations? For a finite population:

  • it is possible to make a list of every member of the population.
  • Population means and variances can be calculated from all the items on the list.
  • Randomness occurs purely by the mechanism we use to sample from the list.
  • There is no “model” (e.g. assumption that population values are normally distributed). Statistical properties of estimators can be derived from first principles, through consideration of the sampling mechanism used to select members from the list.

When we refer to an infinite population, we are typically assuming a statistical model. This is the approach you will have used in all your other statistics modules. For an infinite population:

  • it is impossible to make a list of every member of the population. For example, if studying birth weights of new born babies, including those born in the future, it is impossible to make a list of every baby that will ever be born.
  • We cannot make a random selection from all members of the population (e.g. the population includes babies who haven’t been born yet.)
  • We typically assume a statistical model. For example, we might assume that the birth weights of new born babies are normally distributed. Observing a birth weight of a new born baby is thought of as observing a ‘random draw’ from this distribution, rather than making a random selection from a list. We will need to do checks that the statistical model is appropriate.
  • Population means and variances are defined via the statistical model. For example, if a normal distribution is assumed, the population mean and variance are defined as the mean and variance of the normal distribution.
  • Statistical properties of estimators are derived via analysis of the statistical model (e.g. properties of normal random variables).
